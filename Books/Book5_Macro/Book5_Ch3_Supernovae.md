## Gray — Standard Model Reference

A supernova is the explosive death of a massive star. Core-collapse
supernovae occur when a star's core, having exhausted fusable fuel,
can no longer support itself against gravity — the core collapses in
under a second, rebounds, and drives a shockwave that blows off the
outer layers. Supernova nucleosynthesis produces heavy elements
(oxygen, carbon, iron, and elements heavier than iron) that are
dispersed into the surrounding medium — this is the standard
mechanism by which the elements needed for new stars, planets, and
life get produced and distributed.

Standard Model strength: core-collapse mechanics, light curves, and
nucleosynthesis yields are well-modeled and match observation closely.
Standard Model limitation: the exact "revival" mechanism that turns a
stalled collapse-shockwave into a successful explosion is still an
active area of research (neutrino-driven vs. other mechanisms).
