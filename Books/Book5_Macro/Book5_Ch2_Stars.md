## Gray — Standard Model Reference

A star is a massive, roughly spherical body held together by its own
gravity, generating energy through nuclear fusion in its core.
Hydrostatic equilibrium: outward pressure from fusion balances inward
gravitational pull. The photosphere is the visible surface; the
corona (outer atmosphere) is, puzzlingly, millions of degrees hotter
than the photosphere beneath it — the coronal heating problem, not
fully resolved by mainstream astrophysics (leading candidates: magnetic
reconnection, MHD/Alfven wave heating, turbulence).

Standard Model strength: stellar structure, nuclear fusion rates, and
main-sequence lifetimes are extremely well predicted. Standard Model
limitation: the exact mechanism of coronal heating remains an open
problem, with several competing candidate mechanisms rather than one
settled answer.

## 2D One-Wave Interpretation

In 2D, a star is a sustained, high-amplitude Persistent Mode (A-112)
held in place by its own gradient field.

The star does not "burn" in the everyday sense. It is a large-scale
compression structure whose interior pressure and boundary tension
remain in continuous negotiation — the same restoring-response
mechanism (A-105) that holds any Persistent Mode together, at a scale
where the compression is extreme enough to sustain fusion-like
release internally.

The star's visible surface is not its outer limit. It is a boundary
gate (G-715) — stored pressure and magnetic tension can pass through
or reappear above that boundary as outer plasma heating, rather than
all being expressed as surface heat directly.
