## Gray — Standard Model Reference

The electron is a fundamental lepton with no known internal structure.
Mass: 0.511 MeV/c^2. Charge: -1. Spin: 1/2.
The electron is a point particle in the Standard Model — no spatial extent.
Electrons occupy quantized orbitals around nuclei described by quantum numbers n, l, m, s.
The Pauli exclusion principle: no two electrons can occupy the same quantum state.
Electron orbitals are probability distributions, not classical orbits.
The electron's magnetic moment is slightly larger than predicted by Dirac theory —
this anomalous magnetic moment is one of the most precisely measured quantities in physics
(g-factor = 2.00231930436...).

Standard Model strength: electron behavior predicted to extraordinary precision.
Standard Model limitation: why the electron has the mass it does is not explained.
The point-particle assumption leads to infinite self-energy (renormalization required).

## 2D One-Wave Interpretation

In 2D, the electron is a shell mode — a standing wave pattern on the surface of a boundary.

It is not a particle orbiting a nucleus.
It is a stable pressure pattern living on the harmonic shell (D-05) of the atom.

The shell condition in 2D:
2*pi*R = n*lambda
The electron lives where the wave fits perfectly around the boundary circumference.

At n=1: the ground state. Smallest stable shell.
At n=2: first excited state. Larger shell, higher energy.

The electron does not jump between orbits.
It transitions between shell modes — stable standing wave patterns
at different harmonic distances from the nucleus.

The charge of the electron in 2D is the pressure asymmetry at its shell boundary.
The inside of the shell has higher pressure than the outside.
That asymmetry is what we measure as negative charge.

E_vec ~ -nabla P_cushion  (negative: outward pressure = negative charge)
