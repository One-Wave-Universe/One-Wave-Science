## Gray — Standard Model Reference

In quantum mechanics, the measurement problem is one of the deepest unresolved issues.
The Copenhagen interpretation: before measurement, a quantum system exists in a superposition
of all possible states described by the wave function psi.
Upon measurement, the wave function collapses to a single definite state.
The observer (or measurement apparatus) causes the collapse.

The EPR paradox (Einstein, Podolsky, Rosen, 1935): if two particles are entangled,
measuring one instantly determines the state of the other, regardless of distance.
Einstein called this "spooky action at a distance" and considered it unacceptable.

Bell's theorem (1964): no local hidden variable theory can reproduce all quantum predictions.
Bell inequality violations have been experimentally confirmed (Aspect, 1982; Zeilinger, 2022 Nobel Prize).
This appears to confirm nonlocal correlations between entangled particles.

Many interpretations exist: Copenhagen, Many-Worlds, Pilot Wave, QBism, Relational QM.
None is universally accepted.

Standard Model strength: quantum mechanics predicts measurement outcomes with extraordinary precision.
Standard Model limitation: why measurement causes collapse, what constitutes an observer,
and what happens between measurements remains philosophically unresolved after 100 years.
